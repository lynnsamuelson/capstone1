Dalton's Law states that matter cannot be created or destroyed. This is one of the many principles at play when we use chemical equations to describe what is happening in the beaker.


1. Determine the correct formulas for all the reactants and products in the reaction.


For example, let's look at the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen to produce water.

The reactants are: H2(g) & O2(g) and the product is: H2O(l)


2. Write the formulas for the reactants on the left and the formulas for the products on the right with an arrow in between. If two or more reactants or products are involved, separate their formulas with plus signs.


In our example:

H2(g) + O2(g) H2O(l)


3. Count the number of atoms of each element in the reactants and products. A polyatomic ion appearing unchanged on both sides of the equation is counted as a single unit.


In our example:

H2(g) = 2 Hydrogens
O2(g) = 2 Oxygens
H2O(l) = 2 Hydrogens and 1 Oxygen


4. Balance the elements one at a time by using coefficients. A coefficient is a small whole number that appears in front of a formula in an equation. When no coefficient is written, it is assumed to be 1. It is best to begin with an element other than hydrogen or oxygen. These two elements often occur more than twice in an equation. Do not change the subscripts in the chemical formula of a substance.


In our example:

Hydrogen is balanced but oxygen is not. If we put a coefficient of 2 in front of H2O, the oxygen becomes balanced, but the hydrogen is unbalanced. So, if we put a 2 in front of the H2 then the equation is balanced as shown below.


2H2(g) + O2(g) 2H2O(l)


5. Check each atom or polyatomic ion to be sure the equation is balanced.


In our example:

The left side of the equation has: H2(g) = 4 Hydrogens
O2(g) = 2 Oxygens
The right side of the equation has:
H2O(l) = 4 Hydrogens and 2 Oxygens

6. Finally, make sure all coefficients are in the lowest possible ratio.

In our example:

The coefficients are:
2 H2(g)
1 O2(g)
2 H2O(l)

Since H2 and O2 are diatomic gases, the above is the lowest coefficients possible. Another way to think of it is there is not a lower common demoninator between 2, 1 and 2.
